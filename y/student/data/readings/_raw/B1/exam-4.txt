Natural wonder: The Grand Canyon, USA

Native Americans call the Grand Canyon 'mountain lying down'. It is 443 kilometres long and the Colorado River flows there. The canyon is a baby by geological standards — only five or six million years old. But the rocks at the bottom of it are almost two billion years old. {gap:1} The incredible colours of these rocks *fascinate* visitors — over five million of *them* every year. The Grand Canyon is divided into the North Rim and the South Rim. The South Rim is open all year and receives 90 percent of the park's visitors. The South Rim has an airport and rail service. One popular attraction is the Grand Canyon Skywalk. It is a glass walkway that is 4,000 feet (1,220 meters) above the canyon floor in the Grand Canyon West area of the main canyon. Surprisingly, after making the long journey there, the average visitor spends only fifteen minutes looking at the Canyon. Even so, visitors should still be careful: every year an average of five people fall over the edge. Since opening in March 2007, about 300,000 visitors have walked the Grand Canyon Skywalk each year.

Ancient wonder: Petra, Jordan

The city of Petra is more than 2,000 years old, and was once an important commercial city. Temples, palaces, and a huge amphitheatre were all cut out of the cliffs. Their amazing colours give the city its rose-red appearance. {gap:2} It supported around 30,000 people. Historians believe that the citizens of Petra had to leave it around 551 AD and people in the West forgot about this incredible archaeological site, until a Swiss traveller rediscovered *it* in 1812. Even today, Petra *remains* Jordan's top tourist attraction. It has also been the location for a number of Hollywood films, including Indiana Jones and The Last Crusade.

Romantic city: Venice, Italy

What could be more romantic than going down a Venetian canal in a gondola? No wonder Venice is the favourite of lovers all over the world. Gondolas are traditional and symbolic boats which have been used as transport for more than 10 centuries. This amazing city is located on 118 flat islands, and has over 200 canals and 400 bridges. {gap:3} Tourists can visit the Piazza San Marco. It is one of the most beautiful squares in the world with its 500-year-old buildings. *They* can also see the Basilica of St Mark, which is Venice's most famous church. It is the subject of a million photographs and the architectural star of the Piazza San Marco. Lastly, they can enter the Doge's Palace, the home of many of Venice's most famous paintings.

Modern city: Las Vegas, USA

Las Vegas is one of the most exciting cities in the world. Nevada legalized gambling (money games) in 1931, and Vegas became a home to the biggest casinos. This made the nightlife of the city very popular. {gap:4} The business grew so well that Las Vegas became the gambling capital of the world in less than ten years. The 'strip', in the centre of town, is a five-kilometre long main street with the most expensive hotels, including the *replicas* of the Eiffel Tower, the Egyptian Pyramids and ancient Roman Palaces. *It* uses almost 24,000 kilometres of neon lights! Las Vegas is famous for its 'quickie' weddings (both Elvis Presley and Richard Gere were married here). {gap:5} You can even arrange a 'drive-through wedding' and be back in the casinos in an hour!

Beach: Koh Samui, Thailand

Koh Samui is a tropical paradise located in the warm blue waters to the southeast of Thailand. Although *it* is the kingdom's second largest island after Phuket, tourists didn't know about it until a few years ago. For many years this island has been used for fishing, agriculture and coconut farming. Those three supported the local economy. {gap:6} But since the 1980's Samui has relied on tourism for *income*. In Samui, there are hundreds of coconut trees and brightly-coloured flowers. It is surrounded by white sandy beaches and blue seas. Koh Samui has it all: remote beaches, great shopping, first-class restaurants and an exciting night life.
