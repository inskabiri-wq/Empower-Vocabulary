PASSAGE_TITLE: Stress in the Workplace

PASSAGE:
Three out of every four American workers describe their work as stressful. Occupational stress has been defined as a "global problem" by the United Nations' International Labor Organization. There are a lot of physical effects of *this* problem. In addition to this, the economic consequences of this global problem are also alarming. Workplace stress costs U.S. employers $200 billion per year in absenteeism, lower productivity, staff turnover, medical insurance and other stress-related expenses.

Of course, stress is a factor in everyone's life, particularly during major events such as marriage, divorce or buying a home. But many of the most stressful events are related to the workplace: fear of losing your job, changes in your post, changes in financial status, having new responsibilities, having trouble with the boss, variations in work hours or conditions, and vacation dates.

Surprisingly, stress is not always a bad thing. It can increase creativity and productivity. According to Robert Ostermann, professor of psychology at FDU's Teaneck-Hackensack Campus, "No one reaches *peak* performance without being stressed, whether an athlete, an office worker or a manager." The problem *occurs* when stress is so significant.

Ostermann has found that sources of stress change in different professions. For factory workers, stress often is related directly to the work situation, such as dealing with dangerous heavy equipment or working in an uncomfortable environment. In contrast, office workers are more likely to experience stress related inter-personal relationships on the job. "People pressures" such as unclear supervision, tension among team members and fear of dislike can cause stress.

"For many people the *core* of their social life is the people whom they work with," explains Ostermann. "Most people take it personally, but work is not a social situation. When you begin to get some things that are not exactly the qualities of good relationships — such as rumors and power plays among *executives* who are competing for the next promotion — the amount of stress that you get increases."

Occupational stress is not related only to what goes on at work. Conflicts between the demands of the workplace and of home life are increasingly common. One can be stressed when there is an unbalance between family life and work life. According to a survey of the U.S. Department of Labor, 10% of people who are married or living with children under 18 experience *severe* work-family conflict.

In the current "information age" things move faster than ever before with faxes and instant e-mail messages. In some professions people may face more stress due to the fast changes in technology. For example: "Airline pilots are flying more complicated planes, carrying more people under more pressing time demands and with much more airport traffic than ever before," Ostermann says. Technology in automation has allowed employers to get rid of many jobs. *This* causes a fear of being replaced by a machine, and those who keep their jobs must take further education and training. They tend to have a higher level of responsibility, and thus *it* causes greater stress on them.

"Other managerial changes," *he* adds, "especially increased use of part-timers, administrative changes, increased diversity and adjustments in salaries," lead to increased levels of job insecurity. "Now only about half of managers and employees comment that they feel safe in their jobs, 20 years ago this rate was about 80%."

A particularly alarming cause of stress is *aggression* or violence in the workplace. It is estimated by the U.S. Justice Department that each year more than a million people are the victims of violence at work. Aggressive behavior in the workplace ranges from the few "serious instances of physical assault" to the more common 'passive' forms of aggression, such as not sharing the information with other workers, not answering the phone calls and being late to meetings. Although these passive-aggressive behaviours seem to be not very disturbing, Ostermann notes that "when *they* are constantly repeated, they can result in great psychological harm and loss of personal and organizational productivity."

The physical consequences of excessive stress have long been recognized — from heart attacks and strokes to ulcers and other gastrointestinal disorders. Psychologically, stress can lead to depression, anxiety and even panic attacks. 60% of lost workdays each year can be attributed to stress. In addition, an estimated 75 to 90% of visits to health care providers are due to stress-related conditions, costing employers in increased health care costs. Stress also can have a direct effect on the way people handle their jobs. Employees under stress may make more mistakes, have trouble concentrating, become disorganized, become angry or just stop caring about their work. The Wall Street Journal reported that one third of people surveyed considered quitting their jobs because of stress and 14% actually *did it*.

Many people look for help to solve their problems about stress, and employers are responding. "We look for support systems, things that will balance the stress," comments Ostermann. Options include flexible work weeks and telecommuting opportunities. On the physical side, some organizations provide health clubs where employees can work out and keep fit and cafeterias where workers can choose from a healthy range of meals. Most *corporations* make counseling readily available.

Handling stress, however, is a very individual matter. Alice Mills, co-director of counseling services in the Teaneck-Hackensack Campus Wellness Center, suggests several ways individuals can deal with stress. "The first step is to analyze the situation to determine what is causing the stress and what techniques might best suit the individual," she says. Mills notes that there are many relaxation skills including the use of music, meditation, aerobic exercise, muscle relaxation and imagery. Many of these techniques can be learned through books and video- and audiotapes such as those that the Wellness Center makes available to students, faculty and staff through *its* stress lab. Another important way to combat stress is to sharpen one's communication skills. "You need to learn how to speak about your own needs and wants and how to give positive and negative feedback to others," Mills explains. And, perhaps most importantly, "Take care of yourself."
